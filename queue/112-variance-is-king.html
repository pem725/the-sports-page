A Good Week From Your Receiver Is 10× a Bad One. That’s Where the Season Is Decided.

A fantasy matchup is not won by whoever averages the most points. It is won where the two rosters differ — and they differ where the scoring swings. A slot that reliably delivers the same number every week cannot win or lose you anything; it cancels out. The whole game lives in the volatile positions, and most managers spend their energy everywhere else.

Here is a claim that sounds wrong and is not: the highest-scoring player on your roster is often the least important to whether you win this week. Not because points don’t matter — they are the entire currency — but because a matchup is a difference. You win if your total beats your opponent’s, and a total is a sum of slots. Any slot where both of you reliably score about the same amount contributes almost nothing to that difference. It shows up on both sides of the ledger and quietly erases itself. The margin — the thing that actually decides the week — is built entirely out of the slots that swing.

A number that never moves can’t predict anything

This is a general truth about prediction, not a fantasy quirk. A variable can only separate outcomes to the extent that it variesi. If a quantity is the same in every case — a constant — it is invisible to the outcome; it cannot distinguish a winner from a loser, no matter how large or important it sounds. So when you are hunting for what drives a result, you are really hunting for what moves. The inputs with the most variance carry the most information about which way the outcome will fall. Fantasy football simply makes this law impossible to hide, because the score is the sum of its categories — so the swing of the whole is exactly the swings of the parts, added up. The biggest-variance parts dominate. There is nowhere for the lesson to hide.

The floor and the coin flip

Read the chart and the strategy reads itself. A starting quarterback averages around seventeen points and almost never collapses; a good week is only about two and a half times a bad one. That is a high, dependable floor — and precisely because every manager starts a good quarterback, the position barely separates two teams. It is the same steady contribution on both sides, cancelling out. The skill slots are the opposite animal. A running back, receiver, or tight end averages a third of a quarterback’s points, but a good week runs six to ten times a bad one — they bust to almost nothing or explode. That volatility is not a flaw in the data. It is the game. Your week is decided in those bars, and hardly at all in the quarterback’s. And the purest case of all is the one not shown: the kicker, whose bar would be a narrow sliver from six points to ten. It is the highest-floor, lowest-swing slot on the roster — which is exactly why it never decides anything, and why agonizing over which kicker to stream is the least valuable minute a manager spends.

Where the touchdowns hide

It is worth knowing why the skill positions swing, because the answer is the deepest part of the lesson. A running back’s yardage is steady — he reliably gains fifty to ninety yards, a narrow, predictable band. His touchdowns are not. A touchdown is rare, worth six points, and very nearly a coin flip from week to week. Do the arithmetic on a typical starter and the touchdowns are only about a third of his scoring — but they carry as much week-to-week swing as all of his yardage combined. The variance engine of fantasy football is the touchdown, and the touchdown is the least predictable thing on the field. That is the whole tension in one fact: the events that decide your week are, by their nature, the ones nobody can call.

A number that never moves can’t tell you who’s going to win. Variance isn’t noise to be smoothed away — it’s where the outcome lives.

So spend your attention where the swings are

The practical rule falls straight out of the math. Your decisions — a waiver claim, a start-or-sit, a trade — can only change the outcome where the outcome is genuinely in play, and that is the high-variance slots. Winning the kicker matchup by two points, or fretting over a one-point edge at quarterback, is effort spent where the result was never going to turn. Spend it instead where a good call is worth ten times a bad one: the flex, the third receiver, the boom-or-bust back. That is not a fantasy trick. It is how to read any high-stakes decision — find the few inputs that actually move, and put your thinking there.

One honest warning, which is really the beginning of the next lesson. Variance is leverage, and leverage cuts both ways: the boom-or-bust flex is exactly where you win the week and exactly where you lose it. Whether you should reach for that volatility or run from it turns out to depend on something the average hides entirely — whether you walked in as the favorite or the underdog. The favorite wants to smother the variance and lock the win; the underdog needs to crank it up, because only a big swing can save a game they are losing on paper. That is a piece for another day. For now, the one idea worth carrying out of the room is the simplest and the most ignored: do not chase the biggest number. Chase the one that moves. Variance is king.

Notes & sources

Figures are 2024 regular-season weekly fantasy points (standard scoring) for genuine starters — players with at least twelve games — from nflverse weekly data. “A good/bad week” is the mean plus or minus a typical player’s week-to-week swing (one standard deviation): a quarterback runs about 17 points with a swing near 7; a running back about 7 with a swing near 5; receivers and tight ends carry the largest swing relative to their scoring. The touchdown decomposition uses the same starters, splitting each back’s points into yardage and touchdowns.

The general principle — that a predictor only carries information to the extent it varies, and that in an additive score the variance of the total is the sum of the parts’ variances — has its own primer (Variance). A companion caution applies in real prediction: variance is necessary for a predictor, but not sufficient — a variable also has to actually relate to the outcome. Fantasy is the clean case because the categories are the outcome by construction. See the archive for the companion pieces on communicating uncertainty and rates of change.
