Indiana Just Won It All. Its Four-Year Average Still Says .500.

A rolling average is supposed to reveal where a program is really headed — to smooth away the lucky bounces and show the underlying trend. But an average is a rearview mirror: it confirms a rise only after the rise is over. To catch a program in the act, you need its slope. To catch it early, you need the bend in the slope. Here is who is actually moving.

Indiana won the national championship. The Hoosiers went unbeaten, the best team in the country, the story of the season. And their four-year rolling winning percentage — the tidy line most of us would use to describe “how good is this program” — sits barely above .500, in the same neighborhood as a middling bowl team. The average is not lying. It is doing exactly what an average does: three of the four seasons it blends together were mediocre, so a single perfect year drags it up only a little. The number is honest about where Indiana has been. It is nearly blind to where Indiana is going.

That is the trap in reading a program by its level, and it points at a better way to look. Three different questions hide inside “how’s that team doing,” and they have three different answers. Where is the program now — its level, the rolling average. Which way is it moving — its slope, the velocity. And is that motion strengthening or reversing — the bend in the line, the accelerationi. Miss the last two and you are always describing a program as it used to be. Every club drifts back toward its own long-run leveli — but that level itself can be climbing or sinking, and the average is the last thing to notice.

Who’s rising, who’s falling

Slope alone already tells a story the standings hide. Over the last six years the clearest risers are Ole Miss, climbing steadily under Lane Kiffin from the low .400s toward the high .700s; Tennessee, back among the living; Illinois, doubling its win rate; and the great redemption arc of the sport, Kansas — a program whose rolling average sank to thirteen percent around 2017, the worst sustained stretch in major college football, and has since clawed all the way back toward even. The steepest fallers are led by Stanford, whose line is a near-perfect mountain: up to an eighty-five-percent peak in the Harbaugh-and-Shaw years, then a decade-long slide to the high .200s, one of the sharpest declines a blue-blood-adjacent program has managed in the modern era.

The twist the eye test gets backwards

Now the part that should make a Nebraska fan sit up. Ask a hundred people whether the Cornhuskers are a rising or falling program and nearly all of them will say falling, and they will be describing something real: from a seventy-percent power around 2012, Nebraska slid for the better part of a decade down to the mid-.300s. That is the shape everyone remembers. But look at the bend at the end of the line. Nebraska’s slope has flipped from sharply negative to clearly positive — a swing of roughly seven percentage points a year between its old decline and its new climb, the largest such reversal among established programs. By the only measure that describes the present tense, Nebraska is not the sport’s cautionary tale. It is its sharpest turn upward. The long arc says falling; the acceleration says the fall already ended.

Which is the whole point. Velocity tells you which way a team is moving right now; acceleration tells you whether that motion is about to change — and the two can point in opposite directions for years. Indiana’s average still reads .500 while its slope has gone vertical. Nebraska still looks like it’s falling while its slope has quietly turned up. Read a program by where it sits and you are reading a photograph of last season. Read the bend in the line and you are reading the thing itself.

Level tells you where a program has been. Slope tells you where it is. Only the bend tells you where it’s going.

Where the coach shows up

This is also, quietly, the honest answer to the oldest question in the sport: how long before you know what you have in a hire. You do not read it in the record, which is noisy, or in the rolling average, which lags by design. You read it in the bend. Curt Cignetti walked into Bloomington and put a vertical kink in a line that had been flat for two decades — the signal was there in Year One, long before the average admitted it. Lance Leipold’s turn at Kansas took three years to gather but shows up the same way, as a curve that stops falling and starts to climb. A good hire does not announce itself in the level for years. It announces itself, immediately, in the second derivative — which is exactly where a patient fan, and an honest statistic, should be looking.

Notes & sources

Trajectories are each program’s four-year rolling regular-season winning percentage, from season records via CollegeFootballData, over 2006–2025 (the rolling window reaches back to 2003). The analysis covers programs that have been in the top division continuously across the window, so recent arrivals from the lower division are excluded to avoid transition artifacts. “Slope” is the linear trend of the rolling average over the last six seasons; “acceleration” compares the most recent slope to the prior one.

Indiana’s raw win rate ran roughly .33, .25, .85, and a perfect 2025, so its four-year average sits near .60 despite the title — the lag described above. Kansas’ rolling average bottomed near 13% around 2017; Stanford peaked near 85% in the early 2010s before its decline; Nebraska’s recent slope reversal is the largest among long-standing programs. Figures rounded. This piece extends the newsletter’s earlier work on rates of change; see the archive for the companion pieces on regression and on acceleration as a metric.
