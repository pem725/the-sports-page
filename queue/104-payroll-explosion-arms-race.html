In 1989, All of Baseball Spent $351 Million. Today, One Team Spends More.

In thirty-eight years, the sport’s total payroll has gone from $295 million to $6.1 billion — a twenty-onefold rise. The Dodgers alone, in 2026, spend more than every team combined did as recently as 1989. Here is the curve — and why the number is both more and less staggering than it looks.

Take one number and sit with it. In 1989, the twenty-six teams of Major League Baseball spent, together, about $351 million on player salaries — the entire sport, every roster, one summer. In 2026, the Los Angeles Dodgers spend $415 million. One club, one clubhouse, now outspends the whole of baseball from a generation ago. Not the average team of 1989. All of them, added up.

Figure 1 · The payroll explosion · one 2026 team now outspends the entire league of the late 1980s

The engine is compounding

The curve climbs from about $295 million in 1988 to $6.13 billion in 2026 — a factor of nearly twenty-one. The mechanism is the quietest force in finance: compounding. Baseball’s payroll grew roughly eight percent a year, which sounds almost sleepy — a single good raise. But eight percent a year, sustained for thirty-eight years, is not a raise; it is a twenty-onefold avalanche. The line looks like it goes vertical at the end only because that is what steady percentage growth always does when you finally zoom out far enough to see it.

Smaller than it looks — and bigger

Two honest corrections, in opposite directions. First, the number is smaller than twenty-one-fold makes it sound, because a 1989 dollar and a 2026 dollar are not the same dollar. Much of the rise is simply inflation — the shrinking of the currency, not the swelling of the game. Adjust for it — a late-1980s dollar is worth roughly a thirdi of a 2026 one — and the real growth is closer to seven-fold than twenty-one. Still enormous. Still a real arms race. Just not quite the cartoon the raw figure paints.

Second, the number is bigger than a single total reveals, because the money did not spread out evenly — it concentrated. In 1988 the richest team (the Yankees, $18.9 million) outspent the poorest (the White Sox, $5.9 million) by a little over three to one. In 2026 the richest (the Dodgers, $415 million) outspend the poorest (the Marlins, $82 million) by about five to one, and the raw distance between top and bottom grew from thirteen million dollars to three hundred and thirty. The pool got deeper, and the deep end got a lot farther from the shallow.

And for all that — a fifteen-percent edge

Here is the part that turns the arms race from impressive to almost poignant. The companion to this piece measured what all of this spending actually buys, and the answer was: startlingly little. Across four full seasons, a team’s payroll explains only about fifteen percent of how many games it wins. The other eighty-five percent lives in health, development, judgment, and luck — none of it on the market. So the sport has spent thirty-eight years and twenty-one-fold more money to sharpen an edge that was never large. The Dodgers’ $415 million is a genuine marvel of accumulation and a modest thumb on the scale of the standings. The checks got twenty-one times bigger. What they buy on the field barely moved.

One team, in 2026, outspends every team of 1989 combined. It buys them a fifteen-percent edge.

Baseball’s payroll, 1988–2026

None of which is to say the money is fake or the spenders are foolish. Revenue grew with it — television, then streaming, then the sport’s own valuation — and the checks simply followed. It is a nice historical footnote that the game’s highest-paid player has ranged from Bobby Bonilla in the early 1990s to Alex Rodriguez across the 2000s to Kyle Tucker today; the names change, the ceiling only rises. But when the next franchise raises a trophy and the reflex is “of course, look at the payroll,” keep both halves of the arithmetic in view. The payroll is real, and enormous, and — the standings keep insisting — only about fifteen percent of the story.
