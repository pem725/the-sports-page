They Called It “a Fool’s Errand.” Then They Ranked 138 of Them.

A new list ranks every projected college quarterback from 1 to 138 — and it opens by admitting the whole exercise is guesswork. It is right to admit it. The list refutes itself twice over: the scouts don’t agree with each other, and even if they did, you cannot rank a quarterback out of the offense he plays in.

Every summer the rankings arrive, and this one is ambitious: not a top 10, not a top 25, but a full census — all 138 projected starters, sorted into a strict order from first to last. The authors are candid up front that projecting quarterbacks is close to a fool’s errand, which is the most honest sentence in the piece. Then they spend the rest of it doing the errand anyway, with the confidence of a number line. Their tool was not a formula or a vote; it was roughly three dozen anonymous coaches, general managers, and scouts, with the tiers drawn wherever the “separations felt natural.” Feel is doing a great deal of load-bearing work here, and it cannot hold the weight.

The rank is a borrowed number

Start with the raters, because the piece hands you the evidence itself. On one quarterback, a Big Ten executive shrugs that he’s “just a game manager” and overrated; on the same player, an ACC staffer says anyone who leaves him out of the top three is simply wrong. On another, a Power Four head coach calls him a little overhyped while an SEC general manager calls him top tier in the country. These are not fine distinctions. They are opposite verdicts on the same tape — and they get averaged into a single, tidy, confident integer. When your instrument gives wildly different readings depending on who holds it, the first thing to report is that the instrument disagrees with itselfi — not to publish the average to the nearest rank. The authors half-know this: they bucket the field into seven tiers, which is a quiet confession that the real resolution is coarse. Then they rank inside the tiers anyway, restoring the false precision with the other hand.

Even if they agreed, they’d be measuring the wrong thing

Now grant the impossible — suppose the scouts all agreed. The ranking would still be broken, because it answers the wrong question. It treats “quarterback quality” as a property of the person, a trait you could bottle and carry from team to team. In football it is nothing of the kind. It is a property of the offense — five linemen, a set of receivers, a scheme, a play-caller, a game script — and the quarterback’s box score is the whole machine’s output, not his alone. Ranking the passer out of that context is like ranking violinists by how good the symphonies soundedi. You cannot recover the soloist from the sound of the orchestra by ear, and no amount of scout-hours changes that.

The list even documents the confound and steps over it. One quarterback slides down a full tier — and in the very same breath the authors note he was pressured on nearly 43 percent of his dropbacks, the highest rate in his conference. That is not a footnote; that is the explanation, sitting right there, uncredited. An honest number would strip out the pressure, the drops, the run-game support, and the scheme, and rank only what is left — the part of a quarterback that travels when he changes teams. And the sport just ran that experiment for free: a whole class of passers switched programs through the transfer portal, the closest thing football has to swapping the orchestra while keeping the soloist. A ranking that ignored that natural experiment was never trying to isolate the player.

Ranking the results, and calling it the quarterback

The last error is the oldest one in sports, and the list is built on it. The quarterback at No. 1 is there in large part because his team went deep in January. Another slides because his team dropped two late games. This is outcome bias with a clipboard: read the result, then pin it on the most visible man and call it a scouting report. The piece’s own opening line gives the whole game away, praising a former two-star who “vaulted his team to a national championship.” Did he vault the team — or did a championship team vault him? The sentence quietly assumes the very thing a real evaluation is supposed to test.

You cannot recover the soloist from the sound of the orchestra. A quarterback’s numbers are his team’s numbers, wearing his name.

The Reader’s Defense

What a ranking would need before you believed it

None of this means quarterbacks are unknowable — only that a strict 1-to-138 order is the wrong instrument, worn with unearned confidence. A defensible version would look different in four ways. It would report a context-adjusted residual: performance after subtracting the line, the weapons, and the scheme — the part that is actually his. It would give a range, not a point — a floor and a ceiling — because coaches care about a quarterback’s volatility, not just his average. It would carry error bars, since No. 7 versus No. 8 would not survive a single resampling of the evidence; rank with uncertainty attached, or do not rank. And it would show a backtest: how well did last year’s tiers predict this year’s results and draft stock? Publish the hit rate. If it lands near a coin flip, the exercise is entertainment — which is completely fine, and a good deal more honest than a number line.

That is the point of reading a ranking like this one with your guard up. It is a fun argument, engineered to make you defend your guy and quarrel with a stranger’s — and there is nothing wrong with the quarrel. The trouble starts when the borrowed precision of “No. 34” gets mistaken for knowledge. Four small habits inoculate you: ask whether the raters even agree, whether the number belongs to the player or his team, whether it is describing skill or just results, and whether anyone has ever checked if last year’s version came true. The fool’s-errand line was the truest thing on the page. The rest is an engine for July.
