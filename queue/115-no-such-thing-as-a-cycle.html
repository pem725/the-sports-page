Twenty-Six Baseball Franchises. Not One of Them Runs in Cycles.

Every fan believes their club rises and falls on some hidden clock — a good decade, a lean decade, a rebuild, a window. It is the most natural story in sports and it is not in the data. I tested all twenty-six long-lived franchises against a proper null and found zero real cycles. What is there instead is simpler, and you can actually use it: every franchise has a level it returns to, and a speed at which it returns.

Here is the belief, stated plainly enough to test. A franchise is supposed to move in waves: it climbs for some years, peaks, decays, bottoms out, and climbs again, on a period you could almost set a watch by. If that were true, the wave would show up in the record. So I took every club with a long continuous history, wrote down its winning percentage for each season from 1970 through 2025, and went looking for the wave.

How you actually test for a cycle

The trap here is that you will always find one if you look casually. Any series with a bit of year-to-year momentum — where good years tend to sit next to good years — produces something that looks like a rolling wave to the eye, with no clock behind it at all. Economists worked this out in the 1920s and it embarrassed a generation of business-cycle theorists: smooth a run of pure noise and you manufacture handsome, entirely fictional cycles.

So the honest test needs a fair comparison. For each franchise I measured how strongly one season carries into the next, then simulated four thousand fake histories with exactly that much carry-over and no cycle whatsoever. Then I asked: is the strongest wave in the real record bigger than the strongest wave those fake histories throw up by luck? That is the whole method. You are not asking “can I see a wave?” — you always can. You are asking “is this wave bigger than the waves randomness makes for free?”

Across twenty-six franchises the answer was no, twenty-six times. One club cleared the five-percent bar — and its supposed cycle had a period of fifty-six years, which is the entire length of the record, so it is a drift, not a cycle. Testing twenty-six teams at that threshold, you expect about one such fluke anyway. There is no clock. There never was.

What is really there: two dials

Killing the cycle leaves something better, because the record does have structure — just not periodic structure. Each franchise is described almost completely by two numbers. The first is its level: the winning percentage it keeps returning to. The second is its persistence: how much of this year's deviation from that level survives into next year. Persistence near zero means every season is a fresh draw. Persistence near one means the club drifts a long way from home before the elastic pulls it back.

Read the Yankees row the way a fan never does. Their famous invincibility is a level, not a streak: since 1970 they return to about .564 — a ninety-one-win pace — and their bad years get erased at an unremarkable rate. The Red Sox are the strange ones. A strong level of .537 with persistence of essentially zero means Boston genuinely has no memory: last season tells you almost nothing about this one. Atlanta is the opposite animal, at 0.73 — when the Braves go somewhere, they stay a while, which is why their history reads as a long famine followed by a long feast and feels the most cyclical of anybody. It isn't. It is just slow.

The rulebook sets the elastic

Now the finding that makes this more than a curiosity. Run the identical calculation on the NFL from 1999 and the whole sport sits somewhere baseball never goes. Baseball's average persistence is 0.42. Football's is 0.16, and several clubs come out negative — actively anti-persistent, where a good year predicts a worse one.

That is not an accident of talent. It is design. Football runs a hard salary cap, a draft in reverse order of standing, enormous shared revenue and a seventeen-game season; every one of those is a machine for erasing last year. Baseball has none of them. So the elastic in football is short and violent, and the elastic in baseball is long enough that a franchise can be recognisably itself for thirty years. When people argue about whether a front office is smart or a market is big, they are arguing about a quantity the league office already largely decided.

There is no clock. There is a level you return to, and a rulebook that decides how fast.

Why this matters outside the ballpark

The mistake this piece is really about is not a baseball mistake. Give anyone a wiggly line — a stock, a crime rate, a nation's mood, a franchise — and they will find a cycle in it, name it, and start forecasting the next turn. The cycle is almost always mean reversioni wearing a costume: extreme years are partly luck, luck does not repeat, and the return toward normal reads as the downslope of a wave that was never there. The tell is that a real cycle predicts when. Mean reversion only predicts which way — and every genuine forecast anyone has ever made about a franchise is the second kind, dressed up as the first.

Which is oddly good news if your team is bad. A cycle would mean waiting your turn. A level and an elastic mean the only two things worth arguing about are whether you can raise the level, and how long the elastic will take — both of which are answerable, and neither of which is on a schedule.

Notes & sources

Season records come from the MLB Stats API, 1901–2025; the analysis window is 1970–2025 and covers the twenty-six franchises with at least forty seasons in it. Persistence is the lag-one autocorrelation of a club’s winning percentage around its own mean — a first-order autoregressive coefficient. The cycle test compares each club’s largest periodogram peak against four thousand simulated series matched on that same persistence and variance, which is the fair null: it already contains all the wave-like wandering that momentum alone produces.

One club came in under five percent, at a period equal to the full length of the record, which is a trend rather than a cycle; across twenty-six tests roughly one such result is expected by chance. The NFL comparison uses public regular-season game results, 1999–2025, and the same calculation. Both leagues realigned, expanded and changed schedule length over these windows, which adds noise but cannot manufacture a periodicity that is absent.
