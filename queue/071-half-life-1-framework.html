A Career Doesn't Decline. It Disappears.

Across 518 hitters and 41 seasons of major league play, the median surviving veteran hits about as well in year 14 as he did in year 4. The decline you think you're watching is mostly attrition — the players who slowed down didn't slow down. They left.

Manny Machado is fourteen years into a major league career and, for a few weeks this spring, his batting average ducked below .200. That dip is what prompted this series. The question was simple. Is he past his prime, or just slumping? And while we are at it — where, exactly, does Pete Alonso sit on the same kind of curve, seven years in?

The standard answer reaches for "aging curves" calibrated to chronological age. We are going to reach for something cleaner. The clock that matters for a ballplayer is not the one on his birth certificate. It is the one that starts ticking at his major league debut.

Why service years, not birthdays

A 30-year-old in his third major league season has a fundamentally different baseball body than a 30-year-old in his ninth. One has 1,200 professional at-bats on him. The other has 4,000, with the bone density and the swing wear that go with them. The user of a "career half-life" framework has to choose a clock that ticks at the right speed. Service years tick at the speed of major-league baseball: same level of competition, same travel, same workload bracket. They begin, for everyone, at debut.

Starting from debut also fixes a different problem. You cannot measure half-life from peak if you do not know when peak occurred. Peak is a retrospective verdict; no one knows in real time that they have hit it. Debut is a date on a card. Pick the knowable starting line. The rest of the framework follows.

Figure 1 · Survival Curves by Position · n=448 careers

What survives in the chart above is the answer to a single question. Of the players who became regulars (a season of 300 plate appearances or more in their first three years), what fraction is still putting up a 300-PA season at each later window? That is the actuarial half-life of a position. The half-life of a watt-hour of strontium-90. The half-life of a clean fastball.

The shape is the same at every position. Flat. Flat. Flat. Cliff. Almost every cohort stays nearly intact through years 7–9. Then attrition starts at 10–12, and the floor falls out between years 13 and 15. By year 16, more than two-thirds of every cohort is out of the lineup entirely.

What happens to the survivors

Here is the counterintuitive finding. Among the players still standing at year 13, production has barely moved.

The median first baseman at years 1–3 puts up an .805 OPS. At years 4–6, .859 — a real peak. By years 13–15, the median surviving first baseman is still hitting .788. A six-percent drop over a decade of major-league wear. The same pattern holds at third base (.738 to .746), at shortstop (.699 to .708), at left field (.782 to .777). The position medians look like a flat line with a tiny dip at the end.

This is not because baseball is kind to its veterans. It is because baseball is not. The players whose bats slowed down were not allowed to keep hitting in major league lineups; they were sent down, released, retired, or wedged into a bench role and dropped from the sample. The 14-year veteran you watch from behind home plate looks productive because the 14-year veterans who became unproductive are not there anymore.

The career is not a slope. It is a runway with a cliff at the end.

The Half-Life Framework

Living cases: Alonso and Machado

Pete Alonso · 1B · year 8
.871
2025 OPS · career .857

Alonso enters his eighth major league season inside the years 7–9 bucket. Median 1B OPS for that window: .854. Median PA: 612. His 2025 line beat both. Survival probability for a first baseman through years 7–9, given that he was a regular at years 1–3: roughly 100 percent. Alonso is not past his prime. By the data, he is sitting on it.

Status: Inside the prime window
Manny Machado · 3B · year 15
.795
2025 OPS · career .830

Machado is in his fifteenth season. He is one of the survivors: roughly 46 percent of the 3B cohort that debuted before him is still a regular at years 13–15. Median surviving 3B OPS for that window: .746. Machado in 2025 was at .795, comfortably above the survivor median. His current spring slump is a story about a single month, against a position where a deeper slump — a bigger one — would not be unusual for the few players still here.

Status: Survivor, still above median

The point is not to forecast either man's next month. It is to give the conversation a coordinate system. "Past his prime" without a position curve is folk wisdom; with one, it is a check you can run on yourself.

The civic-mission note

This piece is, in a sense, a sequel to The Reader's Defense on survivorship bias. The aging-curve question is where survivorship lives. The grizzled veteran you see hitting .280 in his sixteenth season is not evidence that baseball ages gently. He is evidence of the players you do not see — the dozens from his draft class who were already cut.

When a fan, a manager, or a general manager argues that "veterans hold up," ask the second question: which veterans? The ones still here, or all the ones who debuted alongside them?

Notes & sources

Cohort: Every hitter with a qualified season (502+ PA) at any point between the 1985 and 2025 MLB regular seasons. Career stats pulled from the MLB Stats API. Service-year clock starts at MLB debut; in-progress 2026 seasons excluded. "Regular" defined as a 300+ PA season, the conventional everyday-player threshold.

Median sample sizes per bucket range from 31 (catchers at 16+ years) to 272 (shortstops at 7–9 years). The catcher and DH curves should be read with the smallest cohorts in mind — n=15 and n=24 respectively for the at-debut cohort — though the survival pattern at those positions is large enough to clear noise.

The chart uses a zero-anchored y-axis (per the Tufte / Huff rule) and includes every position in the dataset, including those whose curves are noisier than others. No outliers were dropped.
