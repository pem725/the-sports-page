A Draft That Has Chosen One Man Since 2019 — and the Quarterback Who Bet a Year on Being the Second.

Everyone has spent a month arguing about Brendan Sorsby. He is the least interesting party in the affair. The interesting parties are the five institutions that took turns offering him a door, and then, each in its season, declined to open one.
By Sal · Guest Columnist

The Earliest He May Now Reach the NFL — A Year, Gone

There exists, tucked into the operating bylaws of the National Football League, a provision for something called the supplemental draft. It is a second, smaller draft, held quietly in the summer, for players who — through some misfortune of timing or conduct — missed the first one. A back door. A second window. A mechanism of mercy, in theory, for the young man whose circumstances changed after the regular draft had already packed up and gone home. Ah yes. The supplemental draft. The league’s most dependable instrument for doing, in any given year, absolutely nothing at all.

I have been turning the Brendan Sorsby business over for some weeks now — one accumulates these cases the way one accumulates anything worth understanding, which is to say slowly, and against one’s better judgment — and I have arrived at the conclusion that nearly everyone discussing it has been arguing about the wrong man. They want to litigate the quarterback. Was he reckless, was he sympathetic, did he deserve what he got. The quarterback, I am sorry to report, is the least interesting figure in the entire arrangement. The interesting figures are the five institutions — the NCAA, a Texas courtroom, the Big 12, the NFL’s calendar, and that phantom summer draft — which spent the better part of a month presenting this young man with one door after another, and then, each in its own appointed hour, declined to open a single one of them.

Let us begin, as we must, with the part that is not funny, so that we are clear before the sarcasm gets going about where it will and will not be aimed. It will be aimed at institutions. It will not, at any point, be aimed at him.

The First Door, and the Numbers Behind It

The NCAA ruled Brendan Sorsby ineligible after determining he had wagered somewhere near $90,000 on professional and college sports across four years. The volume is the thing that stops you: better than 2,900 separate bets totaling more than $30,000 during his two years at Indiana, and at least 165 more totaling at least $38,000 across his two seasons starting at Cincinnati. Forty of those wagers — placed as a freshman, in 2022 — were on Indiana football, which is to say on the sport he was, at that very moment, a rostered member of. These are not, you will notice, the numbers of a man enjoying himself. They are the numbers of a man who is unwell. He has since been diagnosed with a gambling and anxiety disorder and has completed a thirty-five-day stay at a treatment center in Arizona. One might ask what kind of system waits until a 2,900-bet pattern has fully matured before it intervenes; one might ask, and one would not get an answer. We will leave the young man here, in the one corner of this story where he is owed our quiet, and turn to the machinery.

Because here is the cruelty of his actual situation: on the field, Sorsby is a genuine prospect. Across Indiana and Cincinnati he threw for 7,208 yards and sixty touchdowns against eighteen interceptions, ran for another 1,295 and twenty-two scores, and finished his last college season with twenty-four touchdowns against five picks. No professional team ever looked at that tape and said no. No team was ever given the chance to say anything at all. That distinction — between a man who is rejected and a man for whom no door is ever opened — is the whole of this column.

The Second Door, Painted and Then Sanded Off

The legal choreography deserves a moment, if only for its comedy. On the eighth of June, a Texas state judge granted Sorsby a preliminary injunction forbidding the NCAA from punishing him, and thereby appeared to clear him to suit up for Texas Tech this autumn — minus the opening two games, a penalty his own attorneys had thoughtfully volunteered. The reception among the sport’s coaches and administrators ranged, in the reporting, from stunned to something considerably warmer than stunned. And then, as one does when one has lost a round in a courtroom, the Big 12 simply went and found a different courtroom, filing for a federal injunction asserting its own sovereign right to discipline the man the state judge had just freed. A door opened by Texas; a door re-bolted by the conference. Sorsby, displaying rather more clarity than the institutions circling him, declined to spend his autumn as a legal exhibit and turned, instead, to the professionals. Which brings us, at last, to the door that was never a door.

“A second window, the rule book calls it. I have looked closely at this window. It is a very fine painting of a window, executed on a wall of considerable thickness, and it fools a remarkable number of people who really ought to know the difference.”

The Third Door, Which Is a Painting

The regular draft was long finished. The only remaining avenue into the NFL in 2026 was the supplemental draft — and here we must pause, because almost no one shouting about Sorsby’s “path to the pros” appeared to know what the supplemental draft actually is. It is held purely at the league’s discretion. It occurs only in years when the league deigns to approve eligible prospects. And even when it is convened, teams routinely take no one, because claiming a player obliges you to surrender a pick of equal value in next spring’s real draft — a steep price for a man who arrives, by the very design of the pool, wearing a red flag. The supplemental draft’s most reliable feature, across its long and sleepy history, is that nothing happens.

The reason this is not common knowledge is a small masterpiece of human error, and it has two names attached to it: Terrelle Pryor, taken in the third round of the 2011 supplemental draft, and Josh Gordon, taken in the second round of 2012. Two genuine talents, two memorable selections, and between them a single durable illusion — that the supplemental draft is a real road, because once or twice it carried someone famous. It is the oldest trick the mind plays on itself: we remember the year the door opened and forget the decade it stayed shut. Consider the actual ledger.

Players Selected in Recent NFL Supplemental Drafts
Selections in the NFL’s recent supplemental drafts. The draft is discretionary and frequently produces no picks at all; 2019 (Jalen Thompson, to Arizona) was the last time any player was taken, the 2023 edition was convened and selected nobody, and in 2026 the league declined to hold one — the year a real prospect was actually counting on it. Years not shown had no supplemental draft.

Read it slowly, because it rewards the patience. One man chosen in 2015, out of seven made available. Two in 2018. One — a safety named Jalen Thompson, to Arizona — in 2019, and not one human being since. In 2023 the league went to the trouble of holding a supplemental draft for two eligible players and then, having held it, selected neither: a draft convened, in the year of our Lord 2023, for the express purpose of choosing NO ONE. And in 2026, the year a legitimately draftable quarterback was finally and desperately depending on it, the league reviewed the situation and announced it would not be holding a supplemental draft at all. It would, instead, encourage Mr. Sorsby to prepare for the 2027 regular draft — a full calendar year away — with the unbothered grace of a maitre d’ suggesting you come back next season for the table you reserved tonight.

The Error, Named Without a Lecture

I promised no lectures, and I will keep to it, but the principle deserves stating once, plainly, because it is the only durable thing you can carry out of this sorry episode. The mistake everyone made — the courts, the conference, the commentators, and yes, in the end, the young man himself — was to treat the supplemental draft’s existence as evidence of its odds. “There is a draft, therefore there is a path.” It is the same arithmetic that sells lottery tickets. The ticket exists; people therefore plan, in the soft furniture of their minds, around winning. The honest way to price any rare door is never to ask whether it is there. It is to ask how often, lately, it has actually opened. For this one, the answer was: once, since 2019, and not at all in the season it was needed.

The Verdict

So let us settle, finally, who is on trial here, because it is not the quarterback. Brendan Sorsby did not misjudge his arm, or his tape, or his worth to a professional roster — all of which were, and remain, real. He misjudged the architecture. He counted five doors and believed he had five chances, when in fact he had one hallway, and the hallway was a façade: a courtroom door that a conference could re-lock at will, a conference door the courts could pry back open, and at the very end of it, painted with great craftsmanship onto a load-bearing wall, the supplemental draft — the exit the league maintains precisely so that it may always say an exit exists. The system did not fail Sorsby through malice. It failed him through a thousand small discretions, none of which any single institution was obliged to coordinate with any other, and the sum of which was a young man rendered unemployable in his profession for a year, by no decision that anyone in particular ever quite made.

It turns out the door was painted on. It always was. Brendan Sorsby is merely the man who walked into it hard enough that the rest of us, at last, heard the sound.
