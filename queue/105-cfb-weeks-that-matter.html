The Line on Ohio State–Texas Is a Point and a Half.

Twenty-two times this fall, two playoff contenders will line up across from each other. But not all collisions are equal. The Saturdays that actually decide the bracket are the ones where nobody can call the winner — and there is a formula for exactly how much a game like that is worth.

The books have already opened a handful of 2026 games, and one number stands out. Ohio State, ranked No. 1 in the country by nearly everyone, opens as a 1.5-point underdog at Texas on September 12. A point and a half is oddsmaker shorthand for “we truly do not know.” And here is the counterintuitive part: a game we cannot call is not a nuisance to be avoided. It is the single most valuable game on the schedule — the one that, when it ends, will have taught us the most.

Why the closest game is worth the most

Information has a unit. Flip a fair coin and the result carries exactly one bit — the most a single yes-or-no event can hold — precisely because, beforehand, you had no idea. Flip a coin you already know is weighted 99-to-1, and the result barely registers; you learned almost nothing, because you already knew the answer. A blowout is that weighted coin. When a 30-point favorite wins, the season’s picture does not move, because everyone had already penciled it in. A toss-up between two contenders is the fair coin: whichever way it falls, a genuine question gets answered and the bracket rearranges.

You can put a number on it. Take the win probability buried inside a point spread and run it through the information formula, and every game on the calendar gets a score in bits. A pick’em is worth a full bit. A one-score line, like Ohio State–Texas, is worth about 0.99 — nearly all of it. A two-touchdown favorite? Barely 0.7. A 30-point mismatch rounds to zero. The spread is not a prophecy; it is the market’s best estimate of a probabilityi, and it will drift all summer. But the ranking it produces is stable, and it points somewhere specific.

The weeks that matter

Run the whole 2026 schedule against the contender board from Part II — the frontrunners, the hunt, the dark horses — and twenty-two games pit two of them against each other. Cluster those collisions by week, and the season stops looking like a twelve-week blur and starts looking like a handful of Saturdays that do the real work. Here are the six that carry the most.

One honest caveat: only the September openers carry a betting line this far out. The other collisions are toss-ups on paper — two contenders, a real question — but their exact win probabilities will not be priced until closer to kickoff. When the lines post, some of these will firm up into coin-flips and a few will sag into mismatches. We will grade them, week by week, in the Sunday Editions.

A blowout confirms what you knew. A coin-flip tells you something new. Clear your calendar for the coin-flips.

CFB Roadmap, Part III

If you only clear three Saturdays

Not everyone lives inside college football; some of you are here for the Bills and wander over on rivalry weekend. Fair enough. If you protect only three dates all fall, protect these: September 12, when Ohio State and Texas open as a coin-flip and Oklahoma–Michigan does the same; October 10, when the Red River rivalry and Georgia–Alabama stack the SEC’s heaviest afternoon; and November 28, when The Game and Texas–Texas A&M close the regular season with seeds — and tempers — on the line. Everything in between is texture. Those three are the plot.
