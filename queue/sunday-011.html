When the Whistle Stopped Working.

One week. Three sports-governance institutions, ranked roughly by money in the room. Three different enforcement outcomes. The pattern is now visible enough to give a name to. This Sunday Edition tracks the Sorsby three-piece arc against actual outcomes, grades open predictions from prior weeks, and previews the supplemental-draft week that finishes the story.
By The Sports Page · June 21, 2026 · Sunday Edition No. 11
This week the newsletter ran a three-piece arc on the collapse of NCAA enforcement, the wobbling peer layer that tried to substitute, and the high school institutions that — for now — still hold authority. The arc was built around three claims about how enforcement actually works. Today we check the claims. Where they look right, we say so. Where they look wrong or incomplete, we say that too. Misses are the point of the Sunday Edition.

What We Got Right

The Wins

The Carolina prior held all the way to the trophy. Sunday Edition No. 010 said the Bayesian comp at 75% had not been broken by a mid-series 31% scare, and stayed at the prior. Carolina lifted the Cup. This is the cleanest possible Bayesian win on a multi-week prediction: a structural prior that survived noise and was vindicated by outcome.

The Sorsby arc was published before the news cycle settled into a single take. Most sports columns this week framed Sorsby as an integrity story (Pete Rose lineage, gambling threatens sport). The Sports Page argued, in three parts, that the deeper story is about institutional enforcement collapsing in the regimes where the money is largest, and still working in the regimes where the money is small. That frame has not yet been adopted elsewhere. The Sunday Edition reserves the right to revisit this judgment after Part IV.

What We Got Wrong (or Aren’t Sure About)

The Misses and Uncertainties

The Sorsby Part II peer-enforcement counterfactual was a forced concession. Part II argued that the apparent strength of peer enforcement — Big 12 lawsuit, Nebraska threat — would not have actually held up under stress, because Sorsby withdrew before anyone had to forfeit a TV window. That is a counterfactual the newsletter cannot actually run. The honest read is: we made a strong structural claim about an outcome we did not get to observe. Reasonable readers can disagree about whether the claim was earned.

Over-Reactions and Under-Reactions

Over-Reactions

The 16-3 coincidence piece (#80) earlier in the week leaned on the word “coincidence” harder than the data fully justifies. The matching record happens in 13% of comparable years (3 in 23). It is unusual at the specific value 16-3, but not so unusual that the headline framing was the only honest reading. A more conservative version of the piece would have led with “rare but not extreme” rather than “first ever.” Reasonable editorial choice, but a softer one would also have been defensible.

The Road Ahead

Monday June 22 is the entry deadline for the NFL Supplemental Draft. The draft itself runs in the days after. The Sorsby Case, Part IV — the market verdict — publishes when the draft delivers an outcome. Three outcomes the piece can absorb: an early-round selection (talent > integrity discount, NCAA “win” is hollow), a late-round selection (market prices the discount, NCAA reputation has residual deterrent value), or no selection at all (labor market enforces what the NCAA could not).
