## Single question

Does spending more on offense or defense predict NFL success? And does the answer change depending on whether you're building or contending?

## Thesis

"Defense wins championships" is the most famous cliché in football — and as a spending strategy, the data says it's wrong. Every Super Bowl champion over an 8-year study period spent 50%+ of their salary cap on offense, averaging 56%. Offense spending has a stronger, more consistent correlation with wins than defense spending.

But the nuance makes the piece: for rebuilding teams, offense is the right investment. For contenders who already have the offense, the marginal dollar is worth more on defense. The optimal strategy changes depending on where you are in the competitive cycle — and teams that don't know which phase they're in (hello, Jets) misallocate capital systematically.

## The three-layer argument

### Layer 1 — Salary cap evidence
- Offense spending correlates 0.30+ with wins in 6 of 11 seasons studied
- Defense spending correlates 0.20+ in 7 of 11 seasons but never as consistently strong
- 18 of 32 teams spend 50-60% on offense
- Only 2 teams spend more on defense than offense: Jets (53.7%) and Patriots (53.6%)
- The Jets angle: connects directly to Issue #6 (29% first-round hit rate). Overspending on defense + low draft hit rate = the worst-case scenario.

### Layer 2 — Super Bowl evidence
- Every SB winner over 8-year window: 50%+ on offense, averaging 56%
- Range: 52% (2014 Patriots) to 60% (2016 Patriots, 2019 Chiefs)
- "Defense wins championships" fails the spending test

### Layer 3 — The competitive-cycle nuance
- Rebuilding teams (below cap): offense spending is the key predictor. You need a QB and OL to generate wins from a low base.
- Contending teams (made playoffs last year): defense spending becomes the better investment. Marginal dollar is worth more on D.
- Translation for draft: where a team is in its competitive cycle should dictate whether they draft offense or defense with top picks.

### Draft-capital bonus layer
- Interior OL: most efficient draft investment, outperform expectations in Rounds 1-2
- Edge rushers and CBs: lowest first-round hit rates despite being most expensive defensive positions
- Bengals case study: top-tier defensive draft capital for 5 years, still can't field a competent defense. Draft capital doesn't make tackles.

## Describe / predict / control

- **Describe:** Offense spending correlates more consistently with wins. Super Bowl winners average 56% on offense. Jets spend 53.7% on defense.
- **Predict:** Teams that invest top-10 picks in offensive linemen in the 2026 draft will outperform teams that invest in edge rushers at equivalent positions. Testable over 3 years. (Explicit, dated prediction per accountability pledge.)
- **Control:** Two mechanisms: (a) offensive positions have higher draft hit rates → higher expected value per pick, (b) NFL rule changes have tilted the game toward offense → offensive talent is inherently more leveraged.
