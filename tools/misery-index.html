The Misery Indexa fan’s fever chart — what hope did to you, one season at a time

Fandom is hope minus reality. Each spring you walk in expecting roughly what last year became, nudged by which way it was heading. Then the season happens. When it comes in under what you hoped, that’s misery — the line goes up. When it beats your hope, that’s a pleasant surprise — the line dips below the floor. Here are four of us, 2005 to 2025.

above the line = let down below the line = better than hoped measured in “typical season swings” for that sport

How this is built. Hope for a season is last year’s winning percentage, nudged by its trend; misery is the gap between that hope and what actually happened, divided by how much a typical season swings in that sport — so a 12-game college season and a 162-game baseball season are put on the same footing (an 8–4 Notre Dame year isn’t automatically “wilder” than a 90-win Mets year). Records are real: MLB and NFL game results, and Notre Dame football via the college-football data feed.

The honest part: nobody’s average misery is much above zero, because hope quietly recalibrates — suffer enough and you stop expecting. So no fan base is a bigger sucker over the long haul. The difference is the ride: how far the line swings, and which team keeps yanking it.
