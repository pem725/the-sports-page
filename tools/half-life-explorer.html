How to read this table

Click any column header to sort. Want positions ranked by who survives longest? Click "10–12." Want to see which position washes out earliest? Click "13–15" then click again to reverse.

The "Half-Life" column reports the first window where fewer than half the cohort is still a regular. That number is the position's actuarial expiration date — knowing only what year of MLB service a player is in, you can read off how likely it is that he's still in the lineup.

II. Production by Position × Service Year

Median OPS among the players who are still putting up 200+ PA at each window. Among survivors, careers don't slope — they hold flat then cliff. The decline most fans observe is mostly attrition, not deterioration.

What this tool is — and isn't

It's a descriptive snapshot of the 1985–2025 MLB hitting population, organized by position and by years of major league service. It tells you the typical arc; it can't tell you any individual player's future.

It's also a counter to a common error: "Player X is declining because his age-32 numbers are down." Most players who decline don't decline gracefully — they disappear. The 32-year-olds still in the lineup are mostly the ones who haven't declined yet. That makes the lineup look more durable than the population is.
