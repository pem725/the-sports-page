Nineteen Years. The Buffalo Sabres Are Back in the Eastern Conference Final.

To get here, they snapped the longest playoff drought in NHL history (fifteen years), beat the Boston Bruins in six in the first round (their first series win since the 2006–07 season), and rallied from down 3–2 to win the last two games of the second round, including an 8–3 demolition in the Bell Centre that nobody saw coming. To understand how strange this Buffalo run is, you have to know what came before it. The Sabres, before this spring, were the longest-suffering franchise in the National Hockey League. They had not made the playoffs since 2011. That is fifteen consecutive seasons of watching the postseason on television — an NHL record, broken only when they finally clinched a wild card spot in March of this year. The same franchise had not won a playoff series since their 2007 Eastern Conference Semifinal upset of the New York Rangers, a run that ended in the same Eastern Conference Final they are returning to tonight. Their two Stanley Cup Final appearances, in 1975 and 1999, both ended in defeat. They are one of the original six expansion-era teams that has never won a Cup.

This is the franchise that, as of this morning, is four wins from a Stanley Cup Final.

The First Round: Boston, in Six

The 2026 Sabres entered the playoffs as a wild card team after a regular season that finished better than anyone expected and worse than the analytics suggested. Their first-round opponent was the Boston Bruins, division champions and the team most national pundits had picked to come out of the Atlantic. Buffalo won the series in six games — closing it out with a 4–1 victory in Boston in Game 6 that ended a series-win drought that had been running since the Bush administration.

The drought number, before that night, was nineteen years. The Sabres had won zero playoff series between 2007 and that Game 6 in TD Garden. They had played in zero series during most of that span. Tomorrow morning the question will be how Buffalo, the franchise that for most of a generation could not even get on the dance floor, has managed to climb halfway across it.

The Second Round: A Demolition, Then a Game 7

The Montreal series did not look like this for most of two weeks. Buffalo dropped Games 3 and 4 in Montreal to fall behind 2–1, then lost Game 5 at home to fall behind 3–2 in a series they had been favored to win. They flew back to Montreal facing elimination. They proceeded to win Game 6 by a score of 8–3 in the Bell Centre — the largest road-team margin in any Game 6 in Eastern Conference playoff history. The Canadiens were not just beaten that night; they were dismantled by a Sabres team that, finally, played like the team its underlying numbers had been predicting it could be.

And then tonight. {{G7_NOTE}} The series goes Buffalo’s way 4–3, the longest possible distance any series can travel, and the Sabres advance to the Eastern Conference Final for the first time in nineteen years.

What Awaits: An 8–0 Buggy at the End of the Tunnel

The reward for nineteen years of waiting is the Carolina Hurricanes, who as of this morning are 8–0 in the 2026 playoffs — the first team in the four-round, best-of-seven era to start the postseason with two consecutive sweeps (see Issue #56). Carolina has not played a competitive game since Sunday, April 26, when Tylor Megill of the Mets — sorry, when Tony Stankoven of the Hurricanes — iced the Round 2 sweep against Philadelphia. They will have rested longer than any team still standing. They are the model franchise that has been the analytics-darling pick for six straight springs. They are also a team Buffalo has not played a meaningful game against since the 2006 Eastern Conference Final, won by Carolina in seven games on their way to the Cup.

The series will, if everything plays to form, end with Carolina winning in five or six. The history says Buffalo’s underlying talent is real but not Cup-level real; the Hurricanes’ underlying talent is not just real but actively dominant; the gap is wide and the rest advantage is meaningful. None of that is what the Sabres or their fans will be thinking about when Game 1 drops on Wednesday or Thursday at PNC Arena.

The Honest Statistical Note, and the Honest Human One

Probabilistically, the Sabres are heavy underdogs against Carolina. The historical base rate of a wild-card-seeded team coming from where Buffalo started this spring to win a Cup is, charitably, under five percent. The Sabres’ xG differential through eight playoff games is good, not great. The Carolina matchup is the worst possible draw remaining in the East — the Hurricanes have been the only Eastern Conference team that has consistently outshot, outchanced, and outscored Buffalo at five-on-five through the regular season. The math, in other words, calls this a long road.

That said. The Sabres’ entire 2026 has been the math being wrong. The model said they would not make the playoffs. They did. The model said they would not beat Boston. They did. The model said they would not come back from 3–2 down to Montreal. They did. By the time Buffalo is heavy underdogs against Carolina, the right read is not to look at the probability but at how many similar probabilities the Sabres have already broken through this spring. A team that wins the long-tail outcomes four times in two months may not be a coin-flip team. It may be a team whose underlying probability the analytics community has not yet correctly priced.

For now, after nineteen years, the Sabres play another round.

A reader who follows hockey only intermittently is invited to remember that Buffalo, before this run, had not won a playoff series in the entire run of Mad Men, the entire run of Breaking Bad, and the entire run of Game of Thrones. Eras change. Series-win droughts, until they break, do not. This one just did.
