What We Got Right

The Wins

The Soto injury model was our cleanest hit. We gave a 54.4% probability of 10+ games missed, and he’s on the 10-day IL expected to miss 2–3 weeks. The Bayesian prior for calf tightness correctly weighted the most likely scenario.

The small-sample message has been consistent and correct. We said .500 after 6 games means nothing. The Mets are now 6-4 — the signal is emerging, but slowly, exactly as the model predicted.

The Bichette K-rate call was validated. We flagged 53% as a 1-in-1,430 event and said it wasn’t just noise. His rate has come down to 33% — still double his career average. Something is genuinely different about his swing.

What We Got Wrong (or Aren’t Sure About)

The Misses and Uncertainties

Skenes’ recovery is tracking slower than our Gamma-Poisson model projected. After 2 starts, his ERA is 9.53. Our model assumed a return to his 1.97 career ERA within 4–5 starts. He’s not there yet. The model may have underestimated the psychological weight of a historically bad Opening Day.

The $467M infield headline was accurate but arguably too alarmist. Lindor and Bichette WERE historically cold (1-in-77 simultaneously), but the headline leaned into panic rather than patience. In hindsight, we should have led with “this is noise” rather than the dollar figure. Our own small-sample lesson applies to our own framing.

“The purpose of the Sunday Edition isn’t to celebrate being right. It’s to learn from being wrong. A Bayesian model updates when new evidence arrives. So should a newsletter.”

Over-Reactions and Under-Reactions

Over-Reactions

The $467M infield piece used the contract dollar figure as the headline stat. Effective for clicks, but it framed a statistical noise event as a financial crisis. Both players are recovering — Bichette from .111 to .194, Lindor from .143 to approximately .230. We taught the right lesson (small samples lie) but dressed it in the wrong costume (panic). Going forward: lead with the math, not the money.

Under-Reactions

We may have under-reacted to Skenes’ continued struggles. Two starts in, his ERA is still 9.53 and his WHIP is 1.94. Our Issue #1 analysis focused on denominator leverage — correct — but didn’t sufficiently explore the possibility that the Opening Day disaster reflected a mechanical or confidence issue, not just bad luck. Sometimes a bad start IS just a bad start. Sometimes it’s the beginning of something. We’ll keep watching.
