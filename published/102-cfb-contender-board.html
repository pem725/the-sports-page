Twelve Playoff Spots. Five Real Title Threats.

The twelve-team bracket makes the season feel wide open. It is not. Run the whole field through the two-door test from Part I — talent and maturity — and thirty hopefuls sort themselves into a short list of true contenders, a pack of live dark horses, and a long tail of teams the rankings flatter. Here is the board.

Last time, we built a map: two doors into the College Football Playoff, one marked talent (Bud Elliott’s fifty-percent blue-chip gate) and one marked maturity (returning production, the trait that carried an eight-percent-blue-chip Indiana to a title). This time we run all thirty-odd hopefuls through those doors and see where they land. Expansion to twelve teams did something sneaky to the sport’s psychology: it convinced roughly two dozen fan bases they are contenders. The board disagrees.

The frontrunners: five who clear both gates

Only five teams pass the talent test and the maturity test, and they are the only five with an honest claim on the trophy. Ohio State opens No. 1 in both SP+ and the FPI — blue-chip everywhere, and no longer young. Oregon returns roughly two hundred career starts and is the number the models keep telling you to buy. Texas brings back a complete, top-shelf roster. And the team this newsletter follows year-round, Notre Dame, is the cleanest fit on the whole board: the nation’s leader in returning production, stacked with talent, and coached by a program-builder in Marcus Freeman. The fifth is the champion, Indiana — still No. 5 in the ratings, but carrying an asterisk, because the veteran roster that broke the talent rule last year is largely gone, and a title defense on a reloaded team is a different dare entirely.

In the hunt, and the two lanes below

In the hunt sit the usual bluebloods — Georgia, Alabama, Penn State, LSU, Michigan — rosters that clear the talent gate and are one break, a settled quarterback or a defense finding itself, from the top tier. They are dangerous every October. They are also, this specific year, a notch behind the complete five.

The dark horses are where Part I gets interesting, because this is the Indiana lane — maturity without the blue-chip pedigree. Texas Tech is the purest example: a portal-built, veteran, line-of-scrimmage roster with real playoff aspirations, and one glaring hole, since the quarterback seat came open when Brendan Sorsby left amid an eligibility fight. Oklahoma pairs a stingy defense with a healthy John Mateer; Texas A&M is deep and physical under Mike Elko; Tennessee is the quiet one nobody is hyping; Clemson is loaded and underrated. Any of them can crash the bracket. Part I’s caution still applies: crashing the bracket and winning it are different sentences, and the side door has opened exactly once.

Finally, the wild cards — Auburn, Florida, Oklahoma State, Georgia Tech — new coaches and portal classes with the widest range of outcomes on the board. They are the tier where preseason hype most outruns evidence: a real chance to surprise, an equal chance to be the team everyone overrated in July.

Expansion added seats, not title threats. Twelve teams will make the bracket. About five can win it.

What the board is really saying

The honest message of a twelve-team field is not that the sport got more open; it is that it got better at hiding how closed it stays at the top. The bracket now rewards a good regular season with a seat, which is lovely for the middle and almost irrelevant to the trophy, because the title still comes from the small group that clears both gates. This is a board, which means it is a projection, not a promisei; a July depth chart is a rough draft, and one torn ACL rewrites a tier. But the shape is stable, and it points somewhere specific. The one team sitting cleanly at the top of the top tier — blue-chip talent, the most returning production in the country, and a coach patiently building the fundamentals that turn the first two into January — is Notre Dame. If you were going to bet the board, you would bet the corner where both doors open at once.

Notes & sources

Dark-horse and wild-card designations compiled from CBS Sports, Yahoo, and On3 previews; Oklahoma’s John Mateer, Texas A&M’s Mike Elko, and the four new-coach programs (Auburn, Florida, Oklahoma State, Georgia Tech) are drawn from those previews. Texas Tech’s quarterback situation reflects Brendan Sorsby’s reported departure.

This is a tiered board, not a computed ranking: teams are grouped by the maturity-talent framework from Part I plus consensus preseason ratings. Tiers are editorial judgment, and preseason rosters and depth charts shift materially before Week 1. Returning-production and blue-chip figures are as described in Part I (Notre Dame leads returning production; Indiana won the 2025 title at roughly an 8% blue-chip ratio).
