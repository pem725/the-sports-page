The Week in Review

Six issues in six days. Every single one was retrospective, analytical, and deliberately ruthless about how it used the past to describe the present. There were no forward projections this week — no Bayesian posteriors waiting on a sample size, no “check back in six weeks.” The week made a different kind of argument: sometimes the data is already there, the pattern is already clear, and the only honest thing left to do is describe it plainly.

  • #93
    A Career Doesn’t Decline. It Disappears. (Half-Life Series, Part I)
    MLB career survival curves show superstars don’t slow down — they vanish. Median elite-level production ends at age 31.
  • #94
    For Twenty Years You Couldn’t Win the Title Without Elite Recruits. Indiana Won It With an 8% Blue-Chip Ratio.
    Indiana’s 2025 CFP title: 16-0, zero five-star recruits, national talent ranking #72. The first clean exception to a twenty-year iron law.
  • #95
    Every Team Has a Shape. The Mets’ Shape Is Soto, Alone.
    OPS clustering reveals the Mets’ roster is the most top-heavy in baseball: Soto at .906, everyone else averaging .671. The gap is .250.
  • #96
    New Teams Are Supposed to Lose for Years. Las Vegas Won Three Titles in Twenty-Four Months.
    Golden Knights, Aces, Raiders. Three expansion franchises, three championships. The expansion curse is officially deceased.
  • #97
    We Searched 65 Years of NBA Legends for Caitlin Clark’s Twin. The Math Chose Oscar Robertson.
    Euclidean distance across five statistical dimensions finds Oscar Robertson (1961) is Clark’s nearest historical neighbor at d=0.026. Magic Johnson is next at d=0.146.
  • #98
    Host Nations Reach the World Cup Semifinals 59% of the Time. America Is Hosting on Its 250th Birthday.
    Thirteen of 22 World Cups: the host reached the semis. Published July 4. USA plays Belgium in the Round of 16 on July 6. The prior is on the table.

Accountability

What We Got Right

The Mets sell signal (#82) is the clearest hit on the board. The piece ran when the Mets were at 35-48. They are now 36-52 — one win in ten days while Atlanta dropped 14-3 on them July 4. The deadline is August 3. The signal was right, the window is closing, and every additional loss strengthens the case.

The Swanson regression (#90) is tracking correctly on direction. True talent .183 was always a floor estimate; .199 is the current reading. The half-life of overperformance is doing exactly what the model said it would do: bleed slowly, not crash.

The Clark-Robertson piece (#97) held its own epistemically. The article cited partial-season data explicitly and graded itself PENDING before publication. That’s not hedging — that’s the model being honest about what a sample can and cannot tell you.

What We Got Wrong

The Mets clustering piece (#95) described the roster as it was through June 11. Since then, Carlos Mendoza was fired and the lineup has been evolving. The structural diagnosis — Soto alone, everyone else clustered at .671 — remains accurate, but the specific player configuration is in flux. The piece was correct about the problem. It cannot be credited for the solution, which hasn’t arrived yet.

The Sorsby supplemental draft prediction (#83) is now a partial miss that requires a correction issue. The NFL eliminating the supplemental draft removes one of the two enforcement mechanisms the model cited. The Big 12 lawsuit is still live, but a two-variable model with one variable gone is a different model. That piece gets a revision in the queue — see Road Ahead below.

No other forecasting errors to report this week. An all-analytical week produces no new misses.

Over- and Under-Reactions

Over-Reactions This Week

The national reaction to Indiana’s title. Every sports media outlet treated Indiana’s CFP win as proof that the transfer portal democratized college football. The portal was a mechanism, not a cause. Indiana’s roster was assembled primarily through development and scheme. Crediting the portal for Indiana’s title is like crediting the highway system for a road trip — it made movement possible, but it did not choose the destination. The over-reaction obscures what actually happened: a coaching staff built a culture that retained players who would have left under the old rules.

July 4 World Cup coverage. Treating the USA’s group-stage qualification as a semifinal destiny story. The 59% figure is a population average. It includes hosts who went out in the round of 16. It includes Brazil 2014. The prior is informative; it is not a booking.

Under-Reactions This Week

The aging curve implications from #93. The half-life framework is not just a historical curiosity — it is an active roster-construction tool. If elite production reliably ends at 31, every long-term extension signed for a player over 28 is a bet against the median. No front office covered this week pressed that implication. The story was filed under “interesting stats.” It should be filed under “contract risk.”

The Mets’ Soto dependency. The clustering piece ran ten days ago. The Mets have gone 1-9 since. At some point the gap between Soto (.906 OPS) and the remaining lineup (.671) stops being a statistical observation and starts being the entire explanation for the record. No beat reporter has framed it that way yet.

That is not a footnote — that is a fourth outcome the model never considered, and it warrants its own issue to map the new landscape. It publishes Monday.

After that, the Half-Life series continues: Parts II through IV examine career survival curves by position, by era, and then the key question — can you predict the disappearance before it happens? Three analytical issues, each building on the framework established in #93.

The Analog Year series (2021 Mets comp, Astros analog, cross-sport persistence) runs through mid-July. These are the pieces that ask whether this Mets team’s trajectory is predictable from historical precedent. Given the current 36-52 record, the answer is coming into sharper focus.

The calendar’s most important date is August 3: the MLB trade deadline. The sell signal has been live since Issue #82. If the Mets are still at or below .430 win percentage on August 1, the deadline piece writes itself. Two Notre Dame issues are queued for August, timed to CFB camp openings.
